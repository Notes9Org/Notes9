Acute sleep deprivation reduces long-term potentiation in the rodent hippocampus

Mendez, R. & Olufemi, K.· Journal of Neurophysiology· 2021· cited 214· 94% match

Sleep is widely implicated in synaptic plasticity, yet the acute electrophysiological cost of sleep loss remains debated. Here we recorded field potentials in area CA1 of hippocampal slices from rats subjected to 5 hours of total sleep deprivation. Deprived animals showed a 38% reduction in the magnitude of long-term potentiation relative to rested controls, with no change in baseline synaptic transmission. The deficit emerged specifically during the post-learning window and was attenuated following 3 hours of recovery sleep. These findings support a model in which sleep loss selectively undermines the plasticity processes that stabilize newly formed memories.

Directly relevant. This study gives mechanistic, electrophysiological evidence that even short sleep loss weakens the plasticity underlying consolidation. LTP magnitude dropped ~38% after just 5 hours of deprivation1, while baseline transmission was unchanged — pointing to a consolidation deficit rather than a general impairment2.

Timing of memory impairment after total sleep deprivation: encoding versus consolidation

Haas, L. et al.· Learning & Memory· 2023· cited 87· 89% match

We asked whether sleep deprivation disrupts the encoding of new information or its later consolidation. Human participants (n=64) learned a spatial association task either before or after a night of total sleep deprivation. Recall was impaired only when deprivation followed learning, indicating that the consolidation phase is selectively vulnerable. Functional imaging revealed reduced hippocampal–neocortical coupling during post-learning rest in deprived participants. We conclude that the memory cost of sleep loss is dominated by failures of offline consolidation.

Strongly supports a consolidation-specific deficit in humans. Recall fell only when sleep loss came after learning1, and imaging showed weakened hippocampal–neocortical coupling during post-learning rest2 — a human complement to the rodent LTP finding above.

Recovery sleep partially restores consolidation deficits: a meta-analysis

Province, A. & Tan, W.· Sleep Medicine Reviews· 2024· cited 41· 85% match

This meta-analysis pooled 22 studies (1,140 participants) examining whether recovery sleep reverses memory deficits induced by prior sleep loss. Recovery sleep restored an estimated 60% of the consolidation deficit on average, with larger restoration in declarative than procedural tasks. Restoration was incomplete across every included study, and no design eliminated the deficit entirely. The authors caution that "catching up" mitigates but does not abolish the cost of lost sleep.

Adds an important nuance: the deficit is partly reversible, which argues the disruption is functional rather than permanent. Pooled across 22 studies, recovery sleep restored ~60% of the consolidation deficit1 — but never all of it2. Useful as the counter-point in your review's discussion.
