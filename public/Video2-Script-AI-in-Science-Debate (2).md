In one Wiley survey, AI use among researchers jumped from 57% in 2024 to 84% in 2025.

## PART 3 — THE CASE AGAINST (serious, not dismissive) (6:00–9:30)

🎙️ **\[On camera. Tone shifts — slower, fair-minded.]**

> "So… is it case closed? Should everyone go all-in? Not so fast. Because the people raising the concerns aren't tech haters. They're often the \\\*most\\\* careful scientists in the room. And their real concern is much more serious. It’s that AI can make science faster… While making it less reliable. Lets get it some main concerns about AI.

### 

### 3A — Concern 1: It confidently lies (6:20–7:20)

🎙️

> First: AI makes things up. And the scary part is not just that it’s wrong. The scary part is that it’s wrong with perfect confidence.

It doesn’t say:“Hey, I’m guessing here.”

It says: “Here is the answer. Here are the citations. Here is the journal. Here is the DOI.”

And sometimes those sources simply do not exist.



📊 **\[Stat cards:]**

> In one test, GPT-3.5 made up about \\\*\\\*36%\\\*\\\* of the references it gave. \\\*(peer-reviewed study, 2024)\\\*
> Fake citations in real papers are climbing fast: \\\*\\\*1 in 2,828\\\*\\\* (2023) → \\\*\\\*1 in 458\\\*\\\* (2025) → \\\*\\\*1 in 277\\\*\\\* (early 2026). \\\*(Nature analysis, 2026)\\\*
> At a top AI conference, \\\*\\\*100 made-up citations\\\*\\\* slipped past 3–5 expert reviewers, into 53 published papers. \\\*(NeurIPS 2025; arXiv)\\\*



Studies have found that language models can fabricate bibliographic citations, and one analysis of AI-generated scholarly references found large numbers of fabricated or erroneous citations.

And this is not just theoretical.

A 2026 analysis of NeurIPS 2025 papers reported 100 fabricated citations across 53 accepted papers, even after peer review by expert reviewers. Think about that.



This was not a random blog post.

This was one of the most important AI conferences in the world. And fake citations still got through.



Now imagine that happening in biology.

Or medicine.

Or clinical research.

One fake citation may not seem like much. But science is a shared record.

One paper builds on another paper.

One claim becomes someone else’s assumption.

One bad source gets copied into a review, then into a grant, then into another paper.



And slowly, the record gets polluted. That’s why scientists are right to be worried.

Because in science, a confident lie is worse than an obvious mistake.



### 3B — Concern 2: It makes us worse at our own jobs (7:20–8:20)

The second concern is more subtle. It’s not that AI is bad.

It’s that AI might be so useful that humans slowly stop practicing the skill.

This is called deskilling. And we’ve already seen early warning signs.



In a 2025 Lancet Gastroenterology \& Hepatology study, researchers found that continuous exposure to AI during colonoscopy may reduce endoscopists’ performance when they later work without AI assistance.



That is a big deal. Because the point of AI in medicine is not just to help when everything works perfectly. The point is to make care better.

But what happens if the AI is unavailable?

Or wrong?

Or trained on the wrong data?

Or used in a setting where it performs worse?



The human still has to know what they’re doing. Microsoft Research also studied how knowledge workers use generative AI and found that confidence in AI can change when and how people apply critical thinking during tasks.



And that’s the real fear.

Not that scientists will become lazy overnight.

But that, little by little, we outsource the uncomfortable parts of thinking.



The slow reading.

The skepticism.

The “wait, this doesn’t feel right” instinct.

The ability to sit with uncertainty.



And if those skills weaken, science gets faster…But maybe not better.



3C — Concern 3: Where does your data go? (8:20–9:30)

🎙️

> The third concern is privacy.

And for scientists, this might be the most practical one.

Because a lot of research is not public yet. So when someone copies that into a public AI tool, the question is: Where does it go? And the honest answer is: it depends on the tool, the settings, and the agreement.

🎞️ **\[B-roll: a cursor copying text into a chatbot; a "send" click.]**

> "Here's the problem. With most free, public AI tools, whatever you type in can be stored — and used to train the next version of the model. So your unpublished results, your patient data, your lab's big idea… it quietly leaves the building. And you can't take it back."

🎙️

🎙️

> So again, the skeptic side is not being dramatic. If you paste the wrong thing into the wrong tool, you may not be able to undo it. And for a scientist, that is a serious problem."

## PART 4 — WHERE I LAND (my honest take) (9:30–11:00)

🎙️ **\[On camera. Music softens. Warm, direct — the brand moment.]**



So where does that leave us?

Should scientists use AI?

After looking at both sides, I don’t think the answer is simply yes or no.

I think both sides are right.

They’re just answering different questions.

The people excited about AI are right that this is probably one of the most powerful tools science has had in a generation.

It can help scientists move faster.

Read more.

Test ideas sooner.

Find patterns humans might miss.

And maybe even open doors to discoveries that would have taken years otherwise.

Ignoring that completely feels unrealistic.

But the critics are right too.

Because every serious failure we talked about comes from the same mistake:

Letting AI replace the scientist…

Instead of helping the scientist.



AI invents a citation?

That’s a problem if the scientist doesn’t check it.



AI writes a beautiful summary?

That’s a problem if no one reads the original paper.



AI suggests a hypothesis?

That’s a problem if humans treat it like truth instead of a starting point.



AI handles the thinking every day?

That’s a problem if the human stops practicing the judgment needed to catch mistakes.



Because AI can produce an answer. But it still can’t truly know whether that answer deserves to be trusted. You can.



And maybe that’s the line. Scientists shouldn’t avoid AI completely. But scientists also shouldn’t hand over their thinking to it.



Use AI like a co-pilot. Never like the pilot.
